Learning — Turn & River

Turn & River Theory

Barreling logic, river polarisation, blocker concepts, a decision framework, and a forward-looking study roadmap for later streets.

Turn Barreling Logic

A second barrel (turn bet after c-betting flop) should tell a consistent story. The turn card changes the board significantly — a good barrel has a clear reason: improving your range, blanketing villain's draws, or denying equity on a draw-completing card.

Good barrel cards

Cards that improve your perceived range or charge draws heavily.

  • Overcards to board (K on 742)
  • Cards that complete your story (A turn if you rep AK)
  • Blank turns on flush-draw-heavy flops (charge draws)
  • Pairing the board (you rep trips/boat)

Poor barrel cards

Cards that complete draws or hurt your perceived range.

  • Flush-completing cards when you have no flush
  • Straight-completing cards
  • Cards that improve low boards (villain's calling range)
  • Pairing the board when villain is likely full

Sizing on the turn

Turn bets are generally larger than flop bets.

  • Value: 60–80 % pot to deny equity + build pot for river
  • Bluff: 60–75 % pot — fold equity required to be profitable
  • After small flop bet: can use larger turn (range narrows)

River: Value vs Bluff

The river is a binary decision street — draws are resolved. Your range collapses to made hands + bluffs. The key is polarisation: bet big with the nuts and air, check/call or check/fold with medium strength.

💰

River value bet checklist

Am I ahead of villain's calling range? Is the sizing maximising value while staying called? Am I blocking villain's strong continuing hands?

🃏

River bluff checklist

Do I block villain's calling hands? Do I unblock villain's folding hands? Is my bet size consistent with a value bet? Can villain find a fold?

⚖️

Bluff-to-value ratio

At pot-sized bet: ~50% of bets should be value. At ½ pot: ~67% value, ~33% bluffs for villain to be indifferent (minimum defence frequency math).

🔇

Medium hands: check/call or check/fold

Third pair, second pair, weak top pair — these are bluff-catchers. Don't bet them for "thin value" OOP; check and evaluate.

Blocker Concepts

Blockers reduce the number of combos in villain's range. A good river bluff blocks villain's calling hands and unblocks villain's folding hands.

Ace blocker on a flush board

Holding A♠ on A♠7♠2♠ turn bluffs blocks villain's nut flush (A♠X♠). You have fewer combos of nut flush to worry about on villain's side.

Nut blocker for bluffs

If the river completes a nut straight (e.g., T on A-K-Q-J-T), holding the T blocks villain's nut straight — a useful bluff candidate.

Unblocking folds

If you bluff a river that puts 4 to a flush, avoid holding a spade — you want villain to hold missed spade draws that will fold to your bluff.

Pair blockers for calls

Holding top pair when bluff-catching means you block villain's value range (if they bet top pair for value). Adjust calling ranges accordingly.

Advanced Blocker work becomes critical at mid-stakes. For now, focus on the simplest case: ace blockers on flush-completing boards.

Turn / River Decision Framework

Run through these steps on every turn/river decision to slow down and avoid autopilot mistakes.

1

What is my hand strength relative to this runout?

Classify: nut, strong, medium, weak/bluff-catcher, air. Be honest — "top pair" on a four-flush is medium at best.

2

What range did I represent on prior streets?

Your actions told a story. Does continuing to bet (or checking) remain consistent with that story? Incongruent lines are transparent.

3

How does the card change villain's range?

Did the turn/river complete draws? Does it improve villain's calling range more than yours? Update your range reading accordingly.

4

Am I betting for value or as a bluff? Be explicit.

Betting for "protection" or "to find out where I am" are red flags. Every bet should have a clear value or bluff purpose.

5

Does the sizing match my range and objective?

Value bets want to be called → size down on thin value. Bluffs need folds → size up to give poor pot odds. Match size to goal.

Study Roadmap

Turn/river mastery takes time. Here's a phased roadmap to build depth systematically.

Now Basics

Decision framework + pot odds + simple blocker concept. Apply to every tagged hand in session review.

Now HH tagging

Tag all river spots as: value-bet, bluff, call, fold, or mistake. Build a dataset of decisions over 20+ sessions.

Soon Solver intro

Pick one common spot (e.g. BTN c-bet IP, check-back turn, river overbet) and look at a solver tree. Understand polarisation visually.

Soon Barreling frequency

Track your turn barrel rate in HH imports. Compare to solver baseline (~40–50% on most textures). Adjust if significantly off.

Later Range vs range analysis

Import full range vs range boards into a solver or equity calculator. Build intuition for who has the advantage on common runouts.

Later Blocker-led river strategy

Build a systematic approach to river bluff selection based on blockers/unblockers. This requires solid fundamentals first.

Turn / River Study Checklist

  • Run decision framework on every turn spot in session review
  • Classify each river action: value / bluff / call / fold / unclear
  • Identify one river bluff that had bad blockers and one that had good blockers
  • Track how often you barrel the turn after c-betting flop
  • Review every time you bet medium strength hands on the river — were they bluff-catchers?
  • Find one spot where checking back had more EV than betting (protect checking range)
  • Study one solver line for a common board texture — understand polarisation shape
